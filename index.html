FADIR Test (Impingement Test)
Sn 78%Sp 25%FAI
▶
Patient Position

Supine. Examiner on ipsilateral side.

Procedure

Passively move the hip into Flexion (90°), ADduction (across midline), and Internal Rotation (FADIR). Hold 30 seconds. This compresses the anterior-superior acetabular rim and labrum against the femoral head-neck junction. Positive: reproduction of groin/deep hip pain.

Positive Finding

Deep anterior groin pain or the patient's familiar hip pain reproduced. Note: high sensitivity but low specificity — positive in many hip disorders.

Clinical Significance

Screens for Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI) — cam, pincer, or mixed morphology — and labral tears. A positive FADIR with negative distraction test supports intra-articular pathology. High sensitivity makes it a good screening test.

FABER Test (Patrick's Test)
Sn 88%Sp 58%Hip OA / SIJ
▶
Patient Position

Supine. Examiner on ipsilateral side.

Procedure

Place test leg in Figure-4 position (Flexion, ABduction, External Rotation). Ankle rests above opposite knee. Apply gentle downward overpressure on the bent knee while stabilising contralateral ASIS. Measure the distance from bent knee to table and compare sides.

Positive Finding

Groin/anterior hip pain: Hip joint pathology (OA, labral tear, FAI).
Lateral hip pain: Greater trochanteric bursitis / glut tendinopathy.
Buttock/SIJ pain: SIJ dysfunction.
Restricted ROM (knee higher than contralateral): Hip joint stiffness / OA, capsular tightness.

Clinical Significance

Best single test for hip OA (high sensitivity). Reduced ROM indicates hip OA or capsular restriction. Pain location differentiates hip vs SIJ pathology.

Thomas Test (Hip Flexor Tightness)
Sn 89%Iliopsoas / Rectus Femoris
▶
Patient Position

Supine at edge of table. Both knees pulled to chest initially.

Procedure

Patient holds one knee to chest (flattening the lumbar spine). Allow the opposite leg to lower toward the table.
Positive for iliopsoas tightness: Hip of test leg stays in flexion (does not reach neutral 0°).
Positive for rectus femoris tightness: Knee of test leg extends (does not stay flexed).
Measure angle of hip flexion in test leg.

Positive Finding

Test leg fails to reach table (hip remains flexed >0°) = iliopsoas tightness. Knee of test leg extends = rectus femoris tightness.

Clinical Significance

Identifies iliopsoas and/or rectus femoris tightness — common in FAI, hip OA, anterior hip pain, spondylolisthesis, and posterior pelvic tilt compensation. Guides stretching prescription.

Trendelenburg Test
Sn 73%Sp 77%Gluteus Medius
▶
Patient Position

Standing. Examiner observes from behind.

Procedure

Patient stands on one leg (without holding) for 30 seconds. Examiner observes the pelvis level from behind.
Normal: Pelvis on unsupported side rises or stays level (gluteus medius of standing leg is working).
Positive: Pelvis on unsupported (opposite) side drops more than 2 cm below level.

Positive Finding

Pelvis drops on the opposite (non-stance) side — indicates weak gluteus medius on the stance side. Also look for ipsilateral trunk lean (compensatory Trendelenburg sign = severe weakness).

Clinical Significance

Glut medius weakness — common in hip OA, GT bursitis, gluteal tendinopathy, IT band syndrome, and following THR. Correlates with lateral hip pain and valgus knee collapse. Essential for rehabilitation planning.

Ober Test (IT Band / TFL Tightness)
IT Band / TFL
▶
Patient Position

Sidelying with affected side up. Lower hip and knee flexed for stability.

Procedure

Examiner stands behind patient. Stabilise the pelvis with one hand. With the other, flex the upper knee to 90° and abduct and extend the upper hip (to get the iliotibial band behind the greater trochanter). Then RELEASE the leg — allow gravity to adduct the leg. Normal: leg falls/adducts to at least neutral. Positive: leg remains abducted (cannot adduct past horizontal).

Positive Finding

Test leg stays in abduction and does not fall to the table — indicates tight IT band / TFL. Compare sides.

Clinical Significance

IT band tightness is associated with trochanteric bursitis, lateral knee pain (ITBS), and snapping hip syndrome (coxa saltans externa). Guides foam rolling and stretching programmes.

90-90 Test (Hamstring Tightness)
Hamstrings
▶
Patient Position

Supine with hip and knee of test leg flexed to 90°.

Procedure

Hold the thigh perpendicular to the table (90° hip flexion). Ask patient to actively extend the knee as much as possible. Measure the popliteal angle (angle of knee from full extension).
Normal: popliteal angle <20° (knee close to full extension).
Positive (tightness): popliteal angle >20°.

Positive Finding

Knee cannot fully extend — popliteal angle >20°. Greater angle = more tightness. Note: differentiate from sciatic nerve tension (neurodynamic component).

Clinical Significance

Hamstring tightness contributes to posterior pelvic tilt, increased lumbar flexion loading, and hip impingement. Common in athletes, adolescents, and desk workers. Guides hamstring stretching protocol.

Anterior Labral Tear Test
Labral Tear
▶
Patient Position

Supine.

Procedure

Examiner takes the hip into full flexion, external rotation and abduction. Then moves the hip into extension, internal rotation and adduction while applying an axial load through the femur. A positive test produces a click or reproduction of groin pain during this arc.

Positive Finding

Palpable or audible click with reproduction of groin pain during the arc of motion (flexion-ER-ABD to extension-IR-ADD).

Clinical Significance

Suggests anterior acetabular labral pathology. Confirm with MR arthrography. Combined with FADIR, increases diagnostic accuracy for labral tears.

Posterior Labral Tear / Posterior Impingement Test
Posterior Labral / FAI
▶
Patient Position

Supine at edge of table with affected hip hanging off.

Procedure

Patient holds contralateral knee to chest. Allow affected leg to hang in extension. Examiner then moves the hanging hip into extension and external rotation, applying axial compression. Positive: reproduction of posterior hip or buttock pain.

Positive Finding

Posterior hip or deep buttock pain reproduced with extension and external rotation.

Clinical Significance

Suggests posterior acetabular rim impingement, posterior labral tear, or ischiofemoral impingement. Less common than anterior FAI but important in extension-based athletes (dancers, gymnasts, javelin throwers).

Scour Test (Quadrant Test)
Hip OA / Intra-articular
▶
Patient Position

Supine. Hip flexed to 90° with knee bent.

Procedure

Apply axial compression through the femur (push toward the acetabulum). While maintaining compression, rotate the hip in an arc through abduction→flexion→adduction→extension (the "scour" movement). Essentially, the femoral head is being moved around the acetabulum under compression.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of groin pain, deep hip pain, or clunking at a specific arc position. Crepitus indicates degenerative changes (OA). Pain location and arc position indicate pathological zone.

Clinical Significance

Indicates intra-articular hip pathology — hip OA, acetabular cartilage damage, loose bodies, or labral tears. Crepitus is a strong indicator of cartilage degeneration. Guides imaging (X-ray for OA; MRA for labral).

Hip Distraction Test
Intra-articular vs Extra-articular
▶
Patient Position

Supine. Hip and knee extended.

Procedure

Apply traction (distraction) to the hip by pulling the leg distally along its axis while stabilising the pelvis. Hold 30 seconds. If this RELIEVES pain previously produced by impingement tests (FADIR/Scour) — confirms intra-articular pathology.

Positive Finding

Relief of pain with distraction (decompresses the joint) confirms that pain is intra-articular. Absence of relief suggests extra-articular pain source.

Clinical Significance

Key differentiator: intra-articular vs extra-articular hip pain. Positive distraction = labral tear, OA, chondral damage. Negative distraction = tendinopathy, bursitis, muscle tear.

Snapping Hip Assessment (Coxa Saltans)
Snapping Hip
▶
Procedure (by type)

External Snapping (Coxa Saltans Externa): IT band/TFL snapping over GT. Ask patient to replicate the snap by standing and flexing/extending the hip. Palpate over greater trochanter. Positive: palpable/audible snap over GT.

Internal Snapping (Coxa Saltans Interna): Iliopsoas tendon snapping over iliopectineal eminence. Patient sidelying, move hip from flexion/abduction/ER to extension. Positive: snap felt/heard in groin area.

Intra-articular Snap: Labral tear / loose body — deep, often painful click. Assess with FADIR and Scour tests.

Positive Finding

Palpable or audible snapping. Key question: is it PAINFUL? Painless = likely benign. Painful = warrants investigation for labral involvement or bursitis.

Clinical Significance

External: GT bursitis, IT band tightness. Internal: Iliopsoas bursitis/tendinopathy. Intra-articular: labral tear (requires MRA to confirm). Classification guides treatment.

Greater Trochanteric Bursitis / Gluteal Tendinopathy Test
GT Bursitis / Glut Tendinopathy
▶
Procedure

Palpation: Direct palpation over the greater trochanter and its posterior facet. Positive: point tenderness directly over the GT.

Ober Test: IT band tightness increases compressive load on the GT bursa.

Single Leg Stance: Lateral hip pain with prolonged standing on the affected leg.

Cross-leg Test: Sitting, cross affected leg over the other. Lateral hip pain = positive for GT bursitis/tendinopathy.

Positive Finding

Localised GT point tenderness. Pain with single-leg loading or crossing the leg. Lateral hip pain worse after prolonged sitting or walking on uneven ground.

Clinical Significance

Greater trochanteric pain syndrome (GTPS) — umbrella term including GT bursitis and gluteal tendinopathy. More common in women, particularly menopausal. Avoid compressive loads (cross-legged sitting, hip adduction in bed, stretching into adduction).

Stinchfield Test (Resisted Hip Flexion)
Intra-articular / Iliopsoas
▶
Patient Position

Supine with hip flexed to 30–45°, knee fully extended.

Procedure

Ask patient to actively hold the hip at 30–45° flexion. Examiner applies a downward resistance force (toward table) at the knee or distal thigh. Patient resists this force.

Positive Finding

Groin/anterior hip pain: Intra-articular pathology (labral tear, OA, chondral damage, loose body).
Anterior hip/groin pain without intra-articular pain: Iliopsoas strain / tendinopathy.
Weakness without pain = L2-L3 nerve root or hip flexor tear.

Clinical Significance

Differentiates intra-articular from extra-articular (iliopsoas) groin pain. Very useful in clinical practice — easy to perform and interpret.

Log Roll Test
Hip OA / Intra-articularSp 98%
▶
Patient Position

Supine with legs fully extended.

Procedure

Gently roll the entire lower extremity inward (IR) and outward (ER) with the hip and knee extended. This produces purely rotational movement of the femoral head in the acetabulum with minimal involvement of periarticular structures.

Positive Finding

Reproduction of groin/anterior hip pain with log rolling. Highly specific (98%) for intra-articular hip pathology.

Clinical Significance

The most specific single test for hip joint pathology. When positive, highly diagnostic of intra-articular source. Most useful for confirming OA, avascular necrosis, or significant intra-articular disease.
